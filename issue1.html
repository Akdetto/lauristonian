Part 1: Tips from the School Cappies!

By the School Captains

Welcome to the first, inaugural Laurie Leader advice column! We thought there could be no better way to start off than with some spicy advice from your School Captains Tegan and Selina! Shall we begin?

Q: It may only be the beginning of term one but after having two months of lazing around, it can be easy to get into a schoolwork slump. How do you find your motivation?

S: If you have a specific goal that you’re working towards, vision boards can be a really fun way to inspire yourself and be creative! You can put together a collage of images and quotes that embody your vision and motivate you to keep going. Here is my personal one that I keep near my workspace:


Q: With such a morbid moodboard (can you tell we aren't future medical students), what are your tips for staying happy and calm during the term?

S: If you’re someone who rushes around in the morning like me, preparing breakfast the night before is super useful. (Chia pudding or oats with yogurt and fruit are good options.) Generally, having your uniform laid out and everything ready to go guarantees a more restful sleep.

T: During the school year things can get a bit hectic but it’s important to try and stay on top of the workload to avoid everything pilling up. I find that keeping a diary really helps me to stay on top of my work and writing homework/test dates/due dates down immediately when you get them helps to maintain organsatisation. Once you get homework, try and do it as soon as you can to get it out of the way. This way the content will be fresh in your mind so you can complete it most efficiently and effectively.

S: For happiness, this is probably talked about a lot, but being your authentic self is really important. Being authentic and showing vulnerability allows others to connect with you better. In turn, it creates a more genuine environment where everyone can thrive, feeling comfortable to be themselves.


Q: No that's great advice, especially after all the time we spent relying on the internet to communicate in the past couple of years. Tegan, for our last question today, do you have any tips about studying?

It took me a while to find the study technique that worked for me and that was the most effective for learning and remembering material in content heavy subjects. During lockdown, I found that making flashcards and practicing active recall is the best method for me. The Pomodoro Technique is also a great one if you are studying for longer periods of time. Simply explained, this technique involves 25 mins of consistent study with 5 minutes break in between, followed by another 25 minute block. Once repeated 3 times, you should take an hour break away from the books. So, while moving through highschool I recommend trying out different study techniques to see which one works for you so you can be prepared for year 12.

Also, when the workload increases as the year progresses, it can be hard to find balance and leave time for ourselves, especially during exam season. Having a hobby or participating in extracurricular activities really helps to break up the school day and gives you some time away from the screen and the textbooks.


Q: I think the Pomodoro Technique is great as well! There is also a studying technique called Animedoro where you work for 40 minutes and watch 20 minutes of amine in the breaks... although I'm sure the next episode button could get a little hard to resist! Thank you so much to both of your for your thoughts. I'm sure it will have given our readers some ideas that will make for a great start to 2022!

Australia Day and the Alternatives

By Sarah King

These holidays on January 26th, what did you do? As it’s a public holiday, my guess is that most of you reading this would have had a barbeque with family and friends to celebrate our national day.


However, for many First Nations people, this simply isn’t a public holiday. January 26th is the day Australia, or Terra Nullius as it was then known, was colonised. It was after that day in 1788 that Frontier Wars, massacres, and the dispossession of the First Nations began.


Therefore, Australia day is known as Invasion Day or Survival Day for many people. This is also why the campaign to ‘Change the Date’ has become prominent- to make our national day linked to something other than genocide and colonialism.


Now, sorry if I am, in the words of our current Finance Minister Simon Birmingham, “ruining Australia Day for everybody” by talking about its history. Because as our prime minister, Scotty from marketing, reminded us in 2020, January 26th “wasn’t a particularly flash day” for the colonialists either.


However, we should recognise the past atrocities that have taken place on this day. According to a Deakin University survey, 50% of people under 35 also believe this, and want to change the date.


So absolutely, let’s have barbeques and celebrate Australian culture. But let’s do it on a day like January 1st (Federation Day) or May 8th, because it sounds like mate.


Our national day should be something that accommodates our need to eat snags and take a public holiday. But it should also demonstrate that we recognise our past and want to reconcile this. However, the first step is to have an informed opinion and understand why we should Change the Date.
